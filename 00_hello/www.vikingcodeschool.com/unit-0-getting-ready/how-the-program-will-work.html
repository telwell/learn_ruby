How the Viking Program Will Work

Most of what you'll be doing over the next several months is building projects. Everything else we do is to try and get you building as much as possible because that's the best way to learn.

Our teaching philosophy is the "flipped classroom" model -- you will learn the basic material in each unit on your own and then will apply it with the support of your fellow students and instructors.

We'll structure the schedule and the projects with an Agile Development approach so you get to learn how professional teams operate.

In general, our overall approach is:

  1. Learn each section of the current unit's material by using the readings and then see it done in the demos
  2. Build smaller projects at the end of each section to help set the concepts properly
  3. Build larger projects at the end of each unit to challenge yourself to tie everything together and dig deeper.

A Note on Collaboration

We hope you never feel like you're going through the program alone. In fact, we'd love it if you were active in the chat room and engaging with the other students.

In a similar vein, there's really no "cheating" on the coursework. If you and another student want to collaborate on a project, that's encouraged! If you have a friend who does development and can debug some of your thoughts by sitting next to you, great! This kind of collaboration works as long as you're actually learning and not just copying someone else's solution. Were that the case, you'd only be cheating yourself out of the chance to learn, which is why you're here.

The Learning Component

The amount and type of material you need to learn will vary each unit depending on the subject. We'll most often provide custom-created course materials peppered with links to helpful resources and occasionally even videos. We'll have demos spaced throughout as well to provide you with examples of applied concepts and best practices.

The goal is to get you building as soon as possible and we know that you can pick things up quickly so the learning component will usually be pretty dense. This is partly to prepare you for life outside the program, where you'll typically need to pick up new skills by consulting documentation and reading professional blog posts. We'll begin things by helping you build the right conceptual model for a particular section's material and we'll finish by showing you how to extend your knowledge beyond the bounds of the program.

It's okay if you don't fully understand everything on the first pass because you'll get a chance to ask questions and then to apply it in the assignments and projects as well. You'll continue to have access to the course materials after the course has been completed so feel free to stash the links to particularly helpful lessons or those you'd like to come back to.

At times, it may feel like you're "drinking from a firehose" but we've got a lot to cover so it's important that you stick with it and ask questions if you aren't sure about something. That's why we're here! The point isn't to get through each unit's materials by whatever means necessary, it's to really understand what's going on.

The Building Component

The building component comes after you've had a chance to learn the current unit's material.

The first bits of building are the section-ending assignments, which are meant to focus in on that section's concepts. Think of them as a warm-up for the unit-ending project.

The unit-ending project is a larger project that ties together the sections of that unit. These are designed to be fairly significant and typically have additional extensions to allow you to dig deeper.

Submitting Your Assignments and Projects

Everything we do will be run via Github. Your assignments and projects will be posted as repositories on Github which you'll need to fork, complete, and then submit in the form of pull requests. We do not consider an assignment completed until the pull request has been submitted for it.

This workflow will be great practice for what you'll be doing in the "real world" after the program.

Solutions

The solutions to projects are stored in private Github repositories which will become available to you once we've received the pull request with your work. Let us know when you're ready, and we'll enable your access.

Code Reviews

Once you've submitted your work as a pull request, it will be available for others to see. You're encouraged to look through the solutions of other students as well and even comment on them. Learning to read code is as important as learning to write it.

When you meet with us during the week, we'll provide you with feedback on your code. We might leave comments prior to this or walk through your solution with you directly. In either case, the best way to prepare is to read through the solution and come loaded with questions about what we did and how you can make yours better.

A Typical Unit

A unit contains several sections and a major project. Each unit is structured to bring together similarly-themed topics and wrap them in context. The sections are meant to further modularize the content. For instance, in the Algorithms unit, we have broken out sections for File Operations, Recursion, Data Structures and Search Algorithms. Each of those sub-sections contains:

  1. Lessons
  2. A Demo to ease you into the project work
  3. A Test-Yourself section with questions from the reading
  4. An assignment (often something that can be done in an evening)

At the end of each unit, you'll work on a larger project to tie it all together. These will typically take at least a full day to complete and you'll want to make sure you get help on the tricky sections. The projects will typically fall under the Viking Software Consulting theme and are often managed using Pivotal Tracker stories and supporting mockups.

Mentors and speakers will pop in periodically to give group talks and answer questions. We'll let you know when these are scheduled (will typically be outside of US-time-zone work hours).

How to Get Help

Instructors will be available to answer questions via video or chat during most of the week and on weekends. In practice, most questions get answered via chat and we use 1-on-1's to debug particularly thorny issues.

You have the following hierarchy for asking questions:

  1. If you're working with another student on a project, your first line of defense is your partner if you are pairing. We promise they're nice...
  2. Your second line of defense is Google. It's the #1 most used tool in the arsenal of the developer for a reason. If you ask a question that's a very simple Google search away... sorry, but you will probably be ridiculed :) Using Google will also force you to think about your question and how it can best be phrased.
  3. Your teammates are a great resource to ask questions of. They may also be available when the teaching staff is not (e.g. at weird hours of the night). The group chat is typically where most questions get answered and we encourage it because your teammates likely have the same issues as you.
  4. Your Instructors/TAs -- If you let us know when you're working on projects, we'll ask questions to make sure you're on the right track. You're welcome to reach out to us via chat any time and we'll set up a 1-on-1 if you want some more help with a concept or debugging challenge. We're also often available at strange hours because... we have no real lives :)

How To Communicate

We will be using a professional chat and team management tool called Slack. It's a widely used chat application with plenty of added bells and whistles. It only takes a few minutes to figure out, and you'll see how easy it is to get in touch with your instructors and fellow teammates.

Ask your questions in the team chat room. You can easily "call out" an instructor or other students if there's someone specific you'd like to talk to.
